第一篇論文作者：
鄭閔中		 國立陽明交通大學 心智哲學研究所 碩士生 Patient models and the biopsychosocial model in cancer care

The biopsychosocial model (BPSM) is a popular but contentious issue in both clinical practice and philosophy of medicine. From a clinical perspective, it’s well known in medical education that clinicians shouldn’t treat patients as merely biomedical objects but rather a person with biological, psychological, and social dimensions (Gifford, 2017). Yet it’s still more an aspirational goal than one that has been realized in most clinical encounters nowadays. On the other hand, the BPSM has been criticized in philosophy of medicine literature. George Engel proposed the BPSM 40 years ago and hoped it could replace the traditional biomedical model (Engel, 1977). Engel envisioned the BPSM model to be a cognitive model in the mind of the clinicians that not only view the disease as a biomedical phenomenon but also with psychological and social manifestations and causes. However, many philosophers have pointed out that the BPSM has the impractical problem that it’s too vague and unhelpful to guide clinical practice. The BPSM offers little instruction as to how to select and integrate the biological, psychological, and social aspects of the illness condition which may lead to a form of eclecticism in clinical practice (Ghaemi, 2009). The BPSM debate has been revived due to the recent publication of The Biopsychosocial Model of Health and Disease: New Philosophical and Scientific Developments (2019) by Derek Bolton and Grant Gillett (B&D). They propose a solution to the problem of the BPSM by renewing its conceptual and ontological foundations. B&D argue for a metaphysical account of causation across biological, psychological, and social realms with information transferring between these domains and with the 4E (embodied, embedded, enactive, and extended) cognition theory. B&D believe that the metaphysical account could serve as the general model and help scientists and clinicians in the individual disciplines to work out the details of the biopsychosocial causation of each case in their specific contexts. Many philosophers have disapproved of the B&D solution to the problem of the BPSM (Maung, 2021; Aftab & Nielsen, 2021; O’Leary, 2021). Some of which point out that the B&D solution of an abstract metaphysical account still couldn’t address the core issue at stake: how to understand, select and integrate the biological, psychological, and social aspects of the disease and patient in clinical contexts (O’Leary, 2021)? Due to the aforementioned problem, some philosophers have come to a pessimistic account of the BPSM: The BPSM is no more than a general and vague claim that the disease has both biological, psychological, and social aspects of manifestation and causes without providing any concrete suggestion (Roberts, 2023).In this paper, we will address the debate about BPSM from a more practical angle. From the philosophy of science in practice (PSP) perspective (Chang, 2011), we want to understand and construct the BPSM from the details of actual clinical practice. Following recent methodological trends in the PSP community of using qualitative methods in studying scientific activities (MacLeod & Nersessian, 2022), we’ve conducted qualitative research in a cancer care center hospital in Taiwan. From our empirical study, we proposed that clinicians from different disciplines have their own patient models to help them organize the biological, psychological, and social aspects of the disease and the patients. The model has a common central-peripheral structure to help them prioritize the information according to their goal and the distributed cognitive system (Nersessian, 2023) that they operate in, respectively. We will demonstrate how this finding could shed light on the impractical problem of the BPSM by explicating and connecting the patient models from different disciplines.
第二篇論文作者：
郭耘吟		 國立陽明交通大學 心智哲學研究所 碩士生 The intertwined relationship of knowledge and values in Shared Decision-Making

Shared decision-making (SDM) refers to an ideal decision-making model for clinical encounters since 1972. This model emphasizes the joint participation of both practitioners and patients in making treatment or care plans during their decision-making process, especially factoring into the preferences and values of patients.   In this paper, we will focus on SDM in the cancer-care context and use our clinical case studies as supporting evidence for our arguments. Charles, Gafni, and Whelan’s (1997) (CGW, for short) definition of SDM in the cancer-care context was the most adopted one in various SDM guidelines (Carmona C, Crutwell J, Burnham M, Polak L, 2021). Their definition involves the following three conditions: (1) exchange of information between practitioners and patients, (2) both parties share their preferences toward treatment options, and (3) both parties decide on a treatment plan together.However, when the SDM guidelines adopt CGW’s definition, the three conditions are usually modified as the following: (1’) practitioners supply knowledge, including expertise and their experience, (2’) patients disclose their preferences and values in clinical conversation, and (3’) both parties reach an agreement on a treatment decision together. By comparing CGW’s definition and the SDM guidelines, we can see that (1) and (1’) are significantly different because (1’) takes away the patients’ role as providers of information, knowledge, or experience. (2) and (2’) are also different because (2’) does not instruct clinical professionals to express or share their preferences and values.In this article, we will argue that the two modifications made by the guideline versions are epistemically unjust (Fricker, 2007). (1’) is epistemically unjust because it commits to what Fricker called testimonial injustice, a kind of wrong done to patients’ capacity as knowers of their illness experience and daily care practice. (2’) is epistemically unjust because it commits to hermeneutical injustice, a kind of wrong done to some of the clinical practitioners since they do not have the relevant conceptual or interpretative resources under the evidence-based practice paradigm in the medical community.To rectify the epistemically unjust modifications of CGW’s SDM definition, we propose to restore CGW’s original formulations of (1) and (2) and expand them to accommodate current cancer care practice. We will illustrate our proposed SDM model with two clinical case studies to show (a) how to respect patients’ capacity as knowers of their illness experience and daily care practice (b) to recognize different stakeholders’ values embedded in the medical practice, not just patients’ values and (c) to reveal the intertwined relationship of knowledge and values in SDM from both parties.

第三篇論文作者：
嚴如玉		 國立陽明交通大學 心智哲學研究所 副教授 How Values Intertwine with Tools: A Qualitative Case Study of Cancer Care Practice in Taiwan

Philosophy of Science in Practice (PSP) is a recent movement aiming to correct philosophers’ tendency to focus overly on the contents of theories and less on practices (Mitchell, 2003; Kellert, Longino, and Waters eds., 2006; Chang, 2007; Ankeny & Leonelli, 2011 & 2016; Guay & Pradeu (Eds), 2015; Fagan, 2015; Chen, 2015; Yan & Hricko, 2017; Bueno, Chen, and Fagan, eds., 2018; Chen & Hricko, 2019). Chang (2011) defines scientific practice as a three-place relational structure involving agents, activities, and values/goals. His framework directs philosophers’ attention to each component of scientific practice and how they relate. However, what has not been considered in Chang’s framework is the possibility of how values can intertwine with scientific tools. By ‘intertwine,’ we mean values cannot be individuated independently from how tools are individuated. This paper will introduce two methodological approaches for analyzing scientific practice. One is the theory-first approach, and the other is the tool-first approach. We will use a quantitative case study of cancer care practice in Taiwan to argue that the tool-first approach handles the complexity of cancer care practice better than the theory-first approach.PSP needs a metaphilosophical framework to guide their analysis of scientific practice. Particularly, when facing the messy detail of scientific practice in situ, the metaphilosophical framework philosophers use to categorize or individuate entities of scientific practice makes a substantive difference to subsequent analysis. This paper will discuss two metaphilosophical frameworks, each providing a different set of instructions for analyzing scientific practice. The first is the theory-first framework, which instructs philosophers to apply some theory of value to identify tokens of value from the messy detail of scientific practice. The second is the tool-first framework, which guides philosophers to analyze what tools are and how they are used for what purpose. Values are then individuated in relation to how the tools are individuated. In other words, values intertwine with tools.This paper will provide a qualitative case study about the cancer care practice of a Taiwan cancer center. The case study focuses on the value of patient-centered care (PCC) and its relationship to cognitive tools that clinical practitioners use to mediate their interaction and communication with cancer patients. We use three qualitative methods (Merriam & Tisdell, 2015) to collect and analyze our qualitative data. The dataset is from our 125 hours of ethnographical fieldwork in three different types of outpatient clinics and the interview data of 9 clinical practitioners and 8 cancer patients.First, we will use our data to show the differences between theory and tool-first frameworks. That is, how the two frameworks instruct different ways of analyzing our qualitative data. While the theory-first approach instructs philosophers to import theories of persons to inform their analysis, the tool-first approach guides philosophers to identify practitioners’ cognitive tools of persons.Having shown that, we will argue that the theory-first approach falls short of accommodating our qualitative data on three grounds. First, clinical practitioners’ understanding of persons cannot be fully captured by a single theory of persons, be it biomedical theory, psychological theory, or social theory. Second, patients as persons in a clinical context cannot be fully accounted for by a single theory of person. Third, clinical practitioners’ interactions with cancer patients cannot be fully explained by a single theory of persons.We will then argue that the tool-first framework provides better metaphilosophical guidance and generate a better understanding of our qualitative data. By following the tool-first framework, we first identify the clinical practitioners’ cognitive tools of persons, i.e., how practitioners cognitively model their patients as a person in the cancer care context. We call these cognitive tools the “patient models (PMs).” We then show that clinical practitioners working at three different outpatient units and contexts have three types of PMs, each with distinct central-peripheral structures. Finally, we will use these three types of PMs to specify what PPC is in different contexts to support the claim that PCC as a clinical value is individuated by how PMs are individuated.In conclusion, we will discuss our case study’s implication for PSP in general. The implications concern what methods and conceptual tools PSP philosophers can use (or should use) in analyzing scientific practice in situ. We suggest that combining qualitative methods and the tool-first approach is probably the best option.

什麼是「自然」、如何與自然共存，如何實踐自然，是科技與社會領域中具有生動辯論與豐富案例的主題。科技與社會研究強調自然與社會之間關係緊密且相互形塑，而不只是彼此對立、獨立存在的實體（Latour 2005）。值得注意的是，「自然」可能成為新的權威論述，被認為是比較好的、貼近道德的（Daston and Vidal 2004）。像是台灣大家耳熟能詳的「天然欸尚好」，或是人們常說「順其自然」，常是在讚揚原初的狀態，或是減少干涉。然而矛盾的是，當代社會許多強調「自然」的活動，並非完全保持原始狀態或是置之不理，反而需要人們投注心力才可能達成。在當代社會中，人們追求自然的同時也必須積極行動，這是需要持續動態協商的歷程。本場次包含四篇論文，討論孕產、人工生殖與安寧療護的健康照護活動，其中人們如何詮釋「自然」，以及透過什麼樣的努力實踐「自然」。這四篇從孕育生命到生命消逝的案例，幫助我們重新思索行動者與自然之間的關係，以及其中所涉及的物質科技與身體技術。第一篇論文由吳嘉苓分析助產術中的身體技術網絡，凸顯助產師如何透過科技使用和徒手技能，促動產婦的身體實現陰道順產。第二篇由徐蓓婕探討日本社會中被認為相對「接近自然」的輕度刺激試管嬰兒療程，分析科學治理和個人決策在該治療方式中的相互交織。第三篇論文由施麗雯討論自然流產的案例中，醫學論述對女性流產經驗的影響，以及經歷自然流產的女性和伴侶如何理解與詮釋「自然」概念。最後一篇論文由王安琪分析維生醫療科技與安寧照護如何界定「自然死」的界線，並影響人們對於「善終」的論述與實作。關鍵字：自然、照護實作、科技治理、身體經驗、生命週期

本文將助產術（midwifery）視為身體技術網絡，並探查此網絡的物質科技如何運作，促動自然。過去文獻常對比助產模式與產科模式，強調助產模式的徒手操作、重視自然，盡力同理，對比產科模式的預設生產異常，頻繁使用藥物與科技。然而，助產實作亦頻繁使用各類物質科技，以實踐其理想助產方式，參與所謂「自然」的建立。本文提出檢視助產實作，特別是關注物質科技的角度，以了解助產術的特性，以及跟自然的關係。本文提出三類型助產實作，如何與物質共同促成助產術。（一）以物質科技擴展身體。助產師常避免使用侵入性措施，並透過物質科技來擴展身體。例如，相較於主流產科常以藥物催生，助產師強調等待生產「自然啟動」的時機，並以挖掘身體姿勢來尋找最能「促進產程」的方式。因此，助產術的「自然」產程是積極行動所促成。就姿勢調整而言，助產師常採用產球、產椅與瑜伽磚來協助產婦建立不一樣的身體姿勢，以溫水沖泡腹部來減緩疼痛。生產教育的方式，也會以接觸冰塊作為模擬疼痛的感受，作為擴展身體的感受與佈局。（二）以監測科技探索身體訊息。體溫計、血壓計、可攜式的胎心音監測器—杜普勒（doppler）等等，是助產包內理解身體訊息所仰賴的基本儀器。這些有些是與醫院所共享的科技（例如體溫計），但有些特別強掉可攜式，以維持產婦身體的自由體位，並促成助產術強調的移動性。（三）灌注生命力量的科技。即使助產術強調生產是自然的，助產師定位助產工作是執行低風險的孕產照護，仍須備有危急時刻所需的器材與藥物。從事居家分娩的助產人員例行性會準備預防產後大出血的子宮收縮劑，以及緊急狀況提供給母嬰氧氣的甦醒器。然而，這並非表示助產工作僅有這些藥物與器材，才能處理異常產，亦能在沒有新興科技的情況下，以徒手促進子宮收縮，並以徒手進行胎盤剝離。相較於產科醫學的科技使用，助產師就傾向使用簡易科技（產球），攜帶式科技（杜普勒），以能促動更多身體的敏銳度與能量，達成陰道順產的結果。這些科技也多結合—而非取代—助產模型強調的徒手技能、經驗法則。而當原本預設的自然產，出現異常的現象，科技配備也變得更關鍵，但是助產師也仍預想在缺乏科技的情況下，要如何處置，因此保有急救的手法。藉由解析助產術作為身體科技網絡，能夠打破過去文獻對比助產與產科是「徒手vs.科技」、「自然vs.異常」、「經驗vs.學理」的二元對立，更能呈現助產術結合異質元素來豐富技能、促進自然的特性。

游博翔		 國立臺灣大學 地理環境資源學研究所 碩士生 Rescaling Privacy and Controls: Data Governance Regimes in The Cloud Service Industry

Despite the growing awareness of personal data protection across the globe, the multifurcating landscapes of data regulations have aroused concerns of a “backlash” of globalization fueled by ICT advancement in the past decades. These critics consider a variety of regulations on cross-border personal data transfers and data localization legislation detrimental to a globally interconnected digital environment. However, most enterprises who operate their business across borders now rely on cloud-based management service providers (MSPs)—third party companies offering IT infrastructure outsourcing—to maintain their data and sustain cross-border connectivity. These MSPs have developed a series of infrastructural and technical arrangements to ensure their operations in alignment with industry guidelines and local, national, and international rules. Thus, new data governance regimes are emerging as a complex of material, technical, and legal practices to bridge or mediate the inconsistency of different regulatory requirements. This paper investigates how these managerial practices in the cloud industry alter the relations of data to its subject, enterprises, and national/supranational authority. By critically analyzing the technical architectures and discursive structure of their rationale, I indicate two strategies of data governance: (1) scaling up personal data and (2) scaling down controls, which are usually realized by the deployment of machine learning systems and/or encryption techniques. With these rescaling mechanisms, the MSPs produce illusions of sovereignty toward data for their customers as well as the regulatory authority, rendering the exploitation of data value across jurisdictions. Therefore, the topology of digital connections should not be simply evaluated by the static institutional division, but by a more dynamic understanding of practices in data governance.Keywords: data governance, data privacy, data protection law, cloud compliance, digital sovereignty
